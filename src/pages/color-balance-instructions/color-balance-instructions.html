Why Color Balance?

A picture is worth a thousand words, as they say:

The image in the center panel shows the most number of stars against a dark background. The panels at either end look noisy and lack contrast. By following the steps described in this document you will be able to determine that you need to stack 17 frames of red and 14 frames of blue to achieve the appearance of the center panel.

Why does the green stack require the fewest number of frames? It is due to the spectral response of the CCD camera. The Atik 314E is most sensitive to green light and least sensitive to red. On the other hand the Altair 290M is most sensitive to red and least sensitive to blue. This is the reason why you must rerun color balancing for each camera and set of filters.

Why is this important? In astrophotography we strive to capture the faintest wisps of nebulosity in our images. To accomplish this we must stretch the image with software like Photoshop so that outer space is no longer black. Properly exposed red, green, and blue images will show the color of space as it truly is. Sometimes outer space is a dark shade of gray and other times it is a murky red if the frame is within the Milky Way.

With regards to the three-panel image above, if we try to image a galaxy using the same exposure for red, green, and blue, and stack the same number of frames then space will have a magenta cast due to the large amount of noise in the red and blue channels. This is why color balancing is important.

Why HIP-89474?

HIP-89474 is a Sun-like star in the constellation Lyra. It is a Main Sequence star like our Sun having a spectral type of G1V. Our Sun is G2V. For all intents and purposes HIP-89474 is the Sun but seen at a great distance.

What is the color of the Sun? Some say yellow but others say it is by definition "white light". As astrophotographers we need to know which it is so that we can calibrate our camera and filters. We agree with "white light".

HIP-89474 is only visible to northern hemisphere observers and for that matter for only a few months a year. What we need is a catalog of Sun-like stars that can be viewed year-round from anywhere on Earth. You can find a catalog here at the U235 Astrophotography website.

Why AstroImageJ?

AstroImageJ (AIJ) builds upon the robust ImageJ framework providing superb tools for astrophotographers and photometrists alike. It is the "go to" tool for Exo-planet research. AIJ is mature open source software led by Karen Collins, University of Louisville, Kentucky. The online forum is active. Ms. Collins is quick to reply.

For our needs we have these requirements:

  1. Calibration
  2. Registration (AIJ's Alignment)
  3. Stacking
  4. Measurement of a star's brightness

As it is, calibration, registration, and stacking can be found in other software from Deep Sky Stacker (free) to Astro Pixel Processor (paid) and many others. But what stands out for me is AIJ's ability to align frames that are noisy and have only one reference star. Don't try that with other software, some which require a minimum of nine stars. Granted, AIJ will not perform frame rotation but that is not an issue with the short sequences we capture.

The other positive is brightness measurement. Other software provides tools for sampling a single pixel. AIJ provides an aperture over which photons are counted. This yields a stable, accurate measurement not affected by atmospheric turbulence.

Choose a star

Use the table below to choose the range of Right Ascension (RA) given the season. Refer to the G2V Star Database to find stars in that RA range. Data is grouped by northern hemisphere and southern hemisphere, and then sorted by RA. Scrutinize the Declination (DEC) field. Observers in extreme northern latitudes should favor highly positive DEC if possible. Observers in extreme southern latitudes should favor highly negative DEC if possible.

  • Northern Hemisphere observers:
    • Spring: RA from 9h to 15h
    • Summer: RA from 15h to 21h
    • Fall: RA from 21h to 3h
    • Winter: RA from 3h to 9h
  • Southern Hemisphere observers:
    • Spring: RA from 21h to 3h
    • Summer: RA from 3h to 9h
    • Fall: RA from 9h to 15h
    • Winter: RA from 15h to 21h

Good planetarium software is an invaluable tool for visualizing a star field. My personal favorite is C2A.

Determine exposure

Until you get to know your camera choose your green filter. Since most of the stars in the G2V star database are quite bright start with a 5-second exposure. Zoom in on the star and sample the pixels. If the maximum pixel value is between 20,000 ADU and 40,000 ADU then this is ideal. Capture another frame or two and sample again to verify. Increase exposure if the maximum pixel value is below 20,000 ADU. Decrease it if above 40,000 ADU.

Ideally your image acquisition software offers the ability to place a selection box around a star from which you can discover the maximum pixel value. If that is unavailable then most software allows you to hover over a star to read an individual pixel value. If that is the case then be sure to zoom in so as to make it easy to sample each pixel.

Be careful with software that reads out the maximum pixel value of the entire frame. There may be hot pixels and/or brighter stars in the frame that mislead you.

Final checklist

  1. Choose a star.
  2. Slew to the star's coordinates.
  3. Select 1x1 binning.
  4. Determine exposure as described above.
  5. Cover your telescope's objective and capture 50-100 dark frames at your pre-determined exposure.
  6. Cover your telescope's objective and capture 50-100 bias frames at your camera's minimum exposure setting (see note 1.)
  7. Uncover your telescope's objective.
  8. Select your Red filter.
  9. Focus.
  10. Capture a minimum of 10 light red frames at your pre-determined exposure.
  11. Cover your telescope's objective with your electroluminescent panel.
  12. Capture 50-100 flat red frames (see note 2.)
  13. Select your Green filter.
  14. Focus.
  15. Capture a minimum of 10 light green frames at your pre-determined exposure.
  16. Cover your telescope's objective with your electroluminescent panel.
  17. Capture 50-100 flat green frames (see note 2.)
  18. Select your Blue filter.
  19. Focus.
  20. Capture a minimum of 10 light blue frames at your pre-determined exposure.
  21. Cover your telescope's objective with your electroluminescent panel.
  22. Capture 50-100 flat blue frames (see note 2.)
  23. Copy all files to your image processing computer.
  24. Process with AstroImageJ as described in this document (see note 3.)
Notes:

1 For bias frames I use a 1-millisecond exposure.

2 Flat frame exposure times vary from one filter to the next. Adjust the exposure so that the mean pixel value is 30,000 ADU to 40,000 ADU.

3 IMPORTANT: Your red, green, and blue stacks must contain the same number of frames. You may capture more frames but when stacking choose a number and be consistent.

Step 11

Select the Aperture

The aperture has three parts: the inner aperture, the inner annulus, and the outer annulus. The inner annulus is sort of a dead zone which simply separates the other parts. The inner aperture contains the star you want to measure. (Don't make it too small or else photons will fall into the dead zone and be discarded.) The outer annulus contains the sky background. The star's brightness is calculated as the sum of all the pixels in the inner aperture minus the sum of all the pixels in the outer annulus. AIJ calls this Source - Sky in the Measurements table.

The dimensions of the aperture in this screenshot is 20-40-80. (That is the aperture we used for Alignment.) Notice that the outer annulus contains a nearby star that will skew the results. Granted, its effects are minor but you may run into cases where your G2V star is in a crowded space. Refer to the next screenshot.

The dimensions of the aperture is now 15-30-60. As you can see the nearby star no longer affects the outer annulus and the G2V star is fully contained by the inner aperture. We can proceed.

How to use the DP Coordinate Converter

In Step 2: Calibrate Reds the screenshot of the DP Coordinate Converter, reproduced here, has all inputs disabled. That is due to the choice of settings in the CCD Data Processor

Refer to the next screenshot.

Locate the groupbox entitled FITS Header Updates. Make sure that the checkbox for General is checked.

There are two dropdown boxes: Target Coordinate Source and Observatory Location Source. They are set to FITS header target RA/DEC (J2000) and FITS header latitude and longitude, respectively.

One of the dropdown choices is Coordinate Converter manual entry. The purpose of manual entry is to override certain FITS header data that is missing or incorrect. In my case for this dataset of HIP-89474 it contained an incorrect value for Right Ascension (RA).

Refer to the next screenshot.

After selecting manual entry the input controls become enabled.

This screenshot shows that I typed HIP 89474 in the SIMBAD Object ID field. After pressing ENTER other fields updated including the Right Ascension.

Now as we proceed with calibration these coordinates will replace the faulty coordinates in the image FITS header. This is critical for the accurate calculation of Zenith Angle. The U235 Color Balance Calculator relies on this information to correct for the effects of atmospheric scattering of light, also known as Extinction.

Extinction is a function of Right Ascension, Declination, Universal Coordinated Time (UTC), Latitude, and Longitude. Your image acquisition software should fill in this data for you but be proactive and examine the FITS header of at least one of your image files to ensure that this information is accurate.

An alternative to stacking

It may be possible to eliminate Aligning and Stacking (steps 5 through 10) but only if you capture well-exposed red, green, and blue images. By well-exposed I mean a maximum pixel value of 30,000 ADU to 50,000 ADU in your camera's most sensitive channel (mine is green.)

Be sure to measure the brightness of the G2V star in at least three of the calibrated frames for each color. This diminishes the effect of Shot Noise. The U235 Color Balance Calculator enables you to create any number of samples by clicking the New Button.

As always, experiment.
